Understanding Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is one of the most fundamental principles in electrical engineering. Discovered by German physicist Georg Simon Ohm in 1827, it describes the relationship between voltage (V), current (I), and resistance (R) in an electrical circuit:

V = I × R

This means that voltage equals current multiplied by resistance. From this single equation, you can derive any of the three values when you know the other two.

Practical Examples

1. LED Resistor Calculation

To power a standard red LED (2V forward voltage, 20mA current) from a 5V supply, you need a current-limiting resistor:

R = (5V - 2V) / 0.02A = 150Ω

Use the calculator above: enter V = 3 and I = 0.02 to get R = 150Ω.

2. Finding Current Draw

A 12V battery powers a 100Ω heating element. How much current flows?

I = 12V / 100Ω = 0.12A (120mA)

3. Power Consumption

A USB device draws 500mA at 5V. Its power consumption is:

P = 5V × 0.5A = 2.5W
